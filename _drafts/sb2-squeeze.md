Drive south from Austin on I-35 and the development density shifts somewhere around Buda. Fresh concrete sits on a grade-cleared pad to the east. A subdivision sign announces Phase 3 presales. A water tower for a utility district that did not exist five years ago rises above the tree line. The cities out here have been building fast, spending fast, and fielding questions from residents about why tax bills are not falling even as the number of taxpayers has roughly doubled. The answer is buried in a 2019 state law and a single number that governs how much revenue those cities can raise year over year on the property already on their rolls.

## The Revenue Cap

Senate Bill 2, passed by the 86th Texas Legislature and signed into law in June 2019, rewrote the foundational mechanics of property tax administration in Texas. The law, formally titled the Texas Property Tax Reform and Transparency Act, changed several things. The one that matters most for city budgets is the reduction of what used to be called the rollback rate to what is now called the voter-approval rate.

Before SB 2, a city or county could increase its maintenance-and-operations (M&O) levy by up to 8 percent, adjusted for changes in existing property values, before residents could petition for a rollback election. After SB 2, the threshold dropped to 3.5 percent, and if a city adopts a rate above the voter-approval rate an automatic election is required. The calculations are set out in [Chapter 26 of the Texas Tax Code](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/TX/htm/TX.26.htm). The Texas Comptroller's [Truth-in-Taxation publications](https://comptroller.texas.gov/taxes/property-tax/truth-in-taxation/) provide worked examples and the required public-disclosure forms.

The vocabulary changed alongside the rate. The old "effective rate" is now the no-new-revenue rate, or NNR rate. The NNR rate is the tax rate that, applied to the current year's appraisal roll for properties that existed the prior year, would generate the same levy as the city collected the year before. It is not a rate on new construction. It is the rate on existing, previously taxed property. When those properties appreciate in value, the NNR rate falls proportionally. The voter-approval rate is the NNR M&O rate multiplied by 1.035, so the city can raise 3.5 percent more M&O revenue from existing properties than it raised the year before without triggering an automatic election. School districts operate under a parallel structure with a tighter 2.5 percent ceiling, enacted through HB 3 in the same session. Debt service is handled separately; the interest-and-sinking rate that pays bond obligations follows its own voter-approval calculus and is not subject to the M&O cap.

## When the Tax Base Doubles but Revenue Does Not

The counterintuitive feature of SB 2, particularly for fast-growing cities, is that rapid appreciation in existing property values does not translate into proportional revenue growth. It translates into a lower tax rate.

The arithmetic is direct. Suppose a city raised $10 million in M&O levy last year on $1 billion in existing property value, for an effective rate of $1.00 per $100. This year, those same properties are worth $1.1 billion, a 10 percent increase. The NNR rate is now $10 million divided by $1.1 billion, or roughly $0.909 per $100. The voter-approval rate is $0.909 times 1.035, approximately $0.940 per $100. Applied to $1.1 billion, that rate produces $10.35 million in M&O revenue from existing properties. The city collected 3.5 percent more than last year despite a 10 percent increase in the value of existing property. The appreciation is rate-neutral: higher values are matched by a proportionally lower rate, producing only 3.5 percent revenue growth, full stop.

New construction is added to the tax base separately and excluded from the cap calculation. A city with a large new-construction pipeline can generate additional revenue by applying its adopted rate to those new values. But new construction also brings new service demands: lane-miles of road, fire calls, wastewater flows, parks. The net revenue contribution depends on whether new tax revenue exceeds new service cost, and the evidence from fast-growing Texas cities suggests the answer varies considerably by development type and timing.

In Hays County, where population has roughly tripled since 2000, both sides of that equation are moving fast. Existing-property values have appreciated rapidly, the new-construction pipeline has been large, and labor and infrastructure costs have risen with the corridor. The 3.5 percent ceiling binds when per-capita service cost is not falling as fast as the per-capita tax base is growing.

## The New-Construction Escape Valve and Its Limits

Proponents of SB 2 argued that fast-growing cities would have ready access to exactly this escape valve. A large new-construction pipeline compensates for the cap on existing-property revenue. On pure arithmetic, the argument is correct. The question is whether the revenue from new development covers the associated service cost.

The [Texas Municipal League's post-SB 2 analysis](https://www.tml.org/DocumentCenter/View/4138/sb-2-qa_update_jan-2024) documented that high-growth cities often face cost structures driven as much by new service demand as by inflation on existing delivery, and that the 3.5 percent cap creates asymmetric pressure on cities whose costs are scaling with population. Subdivisions requiring new arterial extensions, lift stations, or elevated water storage may not produce net positive fiscal contributions in the first decade of occupancy.

The structural point is that the escape valve is conditional. Cities that rely heavily on new-construction revenue to compensate for the M&O cap are implicitly betting that growth will continue at sufficient pace and that the ratio of new-construction revenue to new-construction service cost will remain favorable. If a development cycle softens, or if the composition of new projects shifts toward lower-value residential, the cap on existing-property revenue tightens with no offsetting new-construction volume.

## Water Debt and the Budget Picture

The [previous post in this series](https://scottlangford2.github.io/scott_langford/posts/2026/05/hays-county-water/) documented how Kyle, Buda, and San Marcos formed the Alliance Regional Water Authority and committed to importing Carrizo-Wilcox aquifer water from eastern Caldwell and Gonzales counties. That imported supply comes with fixed infrastructure costs that now appear in the cities' water-utility funds as long-term debt service obligations baked into the annual financial picture.

Those obligations sit in utility enterprise funds rather than in the general M&O levy. But they matter for the overall budget because they constrain city-wide financial flexibility. When utility debt service is rising alongside a capped M&O levy, the margin for absorbing unexpected cost increases or revenue shortfalls in either fund narrows. Where debt service is growing as a share of the total levy, the M&O margin shrinks further, even before the 3.5 percent cap becomes the active constraint.

## What to Watch

Three things will shape how binding the SB 2 arithmetic is for Hays-area cities over the next few years.

First, the 89th Texas Legislature (2025) included proposals to tighten the cap further. Senator Bettencourt's SB 9 would have lowered the revenue multiplier from 3.5 percent to 2.5 percent for cities with populations of 30,000 or more; the House version proposed 1 percent for all Texas cities. Second, voter-approval elections will reveal how residents in one of the fastest-growing corridors in the country are balancing service-level expectations against tax-bill growth. [VERIFY: whether Kyle, Buda, San Marcos, or Dripping Springs has held or announced a tax-rate ratification election since SB 2 took effect in 2020, and if so, the vote outcome and margin].

Third, the interaction between water conservation and utility revenue deserves attention. Conservation programs that reduce per-account consumption lower utility revenue per customer even as fixed ARWA debt service holds steady. That gap eventually touches city-wide financial decisions. The supply side is covered in the prior post; the demand-management and fiscal sides are where the two stories meet.

Hays County's cities have been running this experiment since 2020. The data are accumulating in annual financial reports, appraisal rolls, and Truth-in-Taxation notices. Tracking how the gap between cost growth and revenue growth evolves under whatever cap the legislature settles on is the work this series is for.
