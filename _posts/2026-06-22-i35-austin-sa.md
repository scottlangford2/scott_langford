If you drive I-35 southbound out of Austin, you can be in downtown
San Antonio in about an hour and twenty minutes. The trip is
eighty miles. For most of the twentieth century, this was a
straightforward run between two mid-sized cities through a thinly
populated farming corridor: cattle ranches, corn fields,
limestone quarries, the occasional small town off an exit. There
were people living along the route, but not very many, and the
economic action was concentrated at the two anchor cities at
either end.

That is not what this drive looks like in 2026. The eighty miles
between Austin and San Antonio are now one of the densest growth
corridors in North America. Kyle, the next town south of Austin
city limits, was a town of two thousand people in 1990. It is
sixty-eight thousand today and growing. New Braunfels has grown
from twenty-seven thousand in 1990 to one hundred and sixteen
thousand. San Marcos has roughly tripled. Subdivision after
subdivision, warehouse pad after warehouse pad, school after
school. The corridor has, in roughly one generation, gone from
*the empty space between Austin and San Antonio* to *the actual
economic engine of South Central Texas*.

![The Austin–San Antonio I-35 corridor]({{ '/images/i35/i35_map.png' | relative_url }})

This post is a short history of that eighty miles. It is not a
comprehensive account — anyone wanting the full version should
read [Schertz](https://www.tshaonline.org/) and Brian Kelsey on
the Austin-SA Megapolitan, the City of San Marcos historical
work, and the Texas State Historical Association entries on each
of the corridor towns. What this post tries to do is something
narrower: trace the corridor's transformation through five
turning points, each of which set up the next, and show what the
numbers look like at each one.

## The five counties

The corridor runs through five counties. From north to south:

- **Travis**, anchored by Austin. Population 1.29 million.
- **Hays**, anchored by San Marcos. Population 241,000 — the
  fastest-growing county per capita in this entire story.
- **Comal**, anchored by New Braunfels. Population 161,000.
- **Guadalupe**, anchored by Seguin and Schertz. Population
  173,000.
- **Bexar**, anchored by San Antonio. Population 2.01 million.

To the east of the corridor, Caldwell County (anchored by
Lockhart) is part of the same economic geography even though it is
not directly on I-35. To the west, the Hill Country counties of
Blanco, Kendall, and Bandera have absorbed some of the same
growth pressure.

The thing to notice about this list is the population gap. The
two anchor counties have almost two million residents each. The
three middle counties have, combined, about five hundred and
seventy-five thousand. Almost all of the growth in this story is
in the *middle* counties — not the anchors. The anchors grew too,
but their growth rates have been steadier and lower. The middle
counties have grown explosively, decade after decade, in a way
that almost no other group of US counties has matched over the
same period:

![Hays leads almost every decade]({{ '/images/i35/i35_growth.png' | relative_url }})

Hays County has posted decadal growth rates above 40 percent in
five of the seven decades since 1960. Comal has done it four
times. Guadalupe three times. Travis County, the anchor, has been
in the 30s and 40s for most of the same period, and Bexar in the
high teens and low 20s. The story of the corridor is the story of
fast small counties pulling up to meet slower large ones.

## Turning point 1: 1956 — the Interstate decision

When the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956 authorized the Interstate
System, the alignment of I-35 was not, in retrospect, obviously
the most economically consequential thing the federal government
did in Texas that decade. But it was. The interstate was sketched
along the existing US 81 alignment, which had been a major
north-south road since the late nineteenth century, when it
followed roughly the path of the old Chisholm Trail.

US 81 went where it went because of geography. The Balcones
Escarpment, the long limestone ridge that defines the eastern
edge of the Texas Hill Country, runs north-south through this
exact swath of land. Towns naturally formed along the escarpment
where springs broke through the limestone: San Antonio at the San
Antonio Springs, New Braunfels at Comal Springs, San Marcos at
the San Marcos Springs, Austin at Barton Springs. The springs
were the original economic asset, and the road connecting them
was the original infrastructure.

The interstate decision in 1956 was, formally, a transportation
decision. In practice it was an economic-development decision
with a fifty-year time horizon. By placing the highway along the
spring line, the interstate guaranteed that the spring-line towns
would have continued access to long-haul traffic and would be the
natural sites for future commercial development.

What the 1956 decision did not anticipate was that the spring-line
towns were not, by themselves, going to be the main beneficiaries.
The main beneficiaries were going to be the open land *between*
the spring-line towns.

## Turning point 2: 1970s — the suburb begins

The first decade in which the corridor's middle counties posted
genuinely accelerated growth was the 1970s. Hays County grew 48
percent in that decade. Comal grew 50 percent. Both numbers were
roughly double what they had been in the 1960s.

What changed was Austin. The University of Texas was expanding
rapidly, the state capital workforce had been growing through the
1960s, and IBM had opened its Austin facility in 1967, followed
by Texas Instruments and Motorola in the early 1970s. Austin's
population went from 252,000 in 1970 to 345,000 in 1980 — a 37
percent increase that was, by Austin's standards before or since,
not even particularly dramatic, but enough to push housing
demand outside the city limits.

Kyle, San Marcos, and Buda absorbed some of that pushed demand.
The growth was not yet *industrial* growth in those towns — they
were not yet manufacturing anything important — but it was
residential growth driven by an Austin commute that was, in
1975, still considered a fairly long drive. Twenty-five miles
down I-35 was the edge of what was reasonable to commute from.

The interesting wrinkle is what was happening simultaneously on
the south end of the corridor. San Antonio in the 1970s was
adding population (Bexar grew 19 percent that decade) but the
adjacent middle counties were not yet responding to it. Comal and
Guadalupe were growing because of New Braunfels' own industrial
base — Mission Drug, Wurstfest tourism, Schlitterbahn would come
in 1979 — not yet because of San Antonio spillover. The corridor
in the 1970s was a two-anchor system with weak coupling between
the anchors.

## Turning point 3: 1990s — Austin tech and the coupling

The 1990s were the decade in which the two ends of the corridor
finally locked together. Austin's tech buildout — Dell expanding
from a Round Rock dorm room into a Fortune 500 company, the
Samsung Austin Semiconductor fab opening in 1996, Motorola and
Freescale scaling — pushed Austin's metropolitan area to 1.25
million by 2000.

Hays County grew 51 percent in the 1990s. Comal grew 53 percent.
Guadalupe grew 39 percent. The growth on this end of the corridor
was now manifestly driven by Austin. Kyle and Buda were no
longer "long commutes" — they were inner-ring exurbs. San Marcos
had begun developing its own employment base (Texas State, by
then growing aggressively as a Hispanic-Serving Institution, the
Premium Outlets opening in 1990) but was still meaningfully
oriented toward the Austin labor market.

Meanwhile, San Antonio in the 1990s was building outward
aggressively along its own commuter rings. The North Side — Stone
Oak, the SH 281 corridor — was where most San Antonio growth
went, not directly up I-35. But Schertz, Cibolo, and Selma —
Guadalupe County's southern edge — were becoming part of the
San Antonio commute shed. Two different metropolitan dynamics
were now reaching toward each other along the same eighty-mile
strip of asphalt.

The 1990s are also when the term *Austin–San Antonio Corridor*
started showing up in regional planning documents. The Greater
Austin Chamber of Commerce and the San Antonio Economic
Development Foundation began publishing joint reports. The
Capital Area Metropolitan Planning Organization and the
Alamo Area MPO began coordinating on I-35 capacity questions.
The interstate was the connective tissue, but the institutional
recognition that the corridor was one economic geography was
catching up to the underlying fact.

## Turning point 4: 2000s–2010s — exurban explosion

The decade and a half from 2000 to about 2015 was when the
middle counties stopped being suburbs of anchor cities and
started being places of their own. Hays grew 61 percent in the
2000s and 53 percent in the 2010s — meaning the county more than
doubled in fifteen years. Comal grew 38 percent and then 49
percent. Even Guadalupe, the slowest of the three, posted 49
percent in the 2000s.

![The corridor's smaller counties grew faster than the anchors]({{ '/images/i35/i35_pop.png' | relative_url }})

Several things happened at once.

First, the Austin housing market detached from local incomes.
The Great Recession briefly flattened prices, but from 2011
onward the trajectory was steep and one-directional. A starter
home in Austin proper became inaccessible to most non-tech
households well before 2015. The pressure to push housing demand
into Hays and Comal counties was relentless.

Second, the I-35 corridor itself became a logistics asset. Amazon
opened a fulfillment center in San Marcos in 2013. The Tanger
Outlets opened in San Marcos in 2017. The Toyota plant in
San Antonio (opened in 2006) created a supplier ecosystem that
spread up I-35 into Guadalupe County. The corridor stopped being
*just* a commuter corridor and became a freight and industrial
corridor.

Third, employment moved south. Where in 1995 the typical Hays
County household worker drove to Austin, by 2015 a meaningful
share of Hays County workers had jobs *in* Hays County. New
Braunfels developed its own employment base. San Marcos doubled
down on Texas State. The corridor became less commuter-dependent
and more economically self-contained — though every measure of
commuting flow still shows the underlying gravitational pull of
the two anchors.

The cumulative effect on output is visible in regional GDP:

![Real GDP in the corridor's smaller counties has more than tripled since 2001]({{ '/images/i35/i35_gdp.png' | relative_url }})

Hays County's real GDP is about three and a half times its 2001
level. Comal's is roughly tripled. Both are well above the
trajectory of either anchor. The corridor stopped being an
appendage of Austin and San Antonio and started being a region
of its own.

## Turning point 5: post-pandemic — the megaregion

The COVID era accelerated patterns that were already underway.
Remote work loosened the binding between residence and Austin
office space; tech-adjacent households that had been priced out
of Austin proper but unwilling to relocate now had a strong reason
to settle in Hays, Comal, or Guadalupe. The 2020 census caught
the first wave of this; the 2023 ACS estimates show it
intensifying.

Traffic counts tell the same story:

![Traffic is heaviest at the anchor cities; middle is climbing fast]({{ '/images/i35/i35_aadt.png' | relative_url }})

I-35 at the anchor-city interchanges is among the most congested
stretches of interstate in the country. TxDOT's I-35 Capital
Express project, working its way through downtown Austin, is the
state's largest urban highway project. But the more interesting
story is in the middle: AADT at Buda, Kyle, and San Marcos is now
in the 140,000–180,000 range, which is what *core urban* highway
counts looked like a generation ago. The middle of the corridor
is filling in.

The Austin–San Antonio MegaRegion, as the joint planning
documents now call it, is increasingly being treated as a single
economic geography for transportation planning, water supply
planning, and workforce planning. The Lone Star Rail proposal —
an Austin-to-San-Antonio passenger rail line that lived for
fifteen years inside the regional planning conversation before
dying for funding reasons in 2016 — was an early acknowledgment
of the corridor as one place. The proposal is starting to come
back around.

## Why this matters for public finance

If you are reading Southbound 35 for the public-finance
content, the corridor story is the context for almost everything
else. Hays County's school finance pressures (covered in [the
Hays growth post]({{ '/posts/2026/04/hays-county-growth/' | relative_url }}) and
[the schools post]({{ '/posts/2026/05/hays-county-schools/' | relative_url }})) are not happening in isolation —
they are happening because of demographic pressures that
originate in this corridor history. Comal's water-supply
constraints (which the next several posts will cover) are a
direct consequence of the same growth pattern. New Braunfels'
governance fights over zoning and short-term rentals are a
consequence of the same.

The corridor is also the context for the property tax mechanics
covered in [the previous post]({{ '/posts/2026/06/property-tax-101/' | relative_url }}). Hays and Comal are
the kinds of fast-growing counties whose appraisal values have
risen most steeply, where the gap between the rate the
homeowner pays and the rate the city or school district *thinks*
they pay is widest, and where the SB 2 levy caps have done the
least to slow individual bills (because new construction is
exempted from the caps and the corridor counties are heavy on
new construction).

The next several Southbound 35 posts will pick up specific
corridor stories: New Braunfels' Edwards Aquifer water rights, the
Schertz–Cibolo–Universal City school district that straddles three
counties, the bond programs that San Marcos is using to finance
its downtown infill. Each of those stories runs on the same
underlying current — eighty miles of interstate that, in one
generation, transformed from connective tissue into the actual
economic spine of South Central Texas.
