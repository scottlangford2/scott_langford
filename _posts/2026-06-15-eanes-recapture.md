In the early 1990s, the Texas Supreme Court ruled that the state's
school finance system was unconstitutional. Property-poor districts
were spending dramatically less per pupil than property-wealthy
ones, even when the poor districts taxed themselves at higher rates.
The state's response, eventually, was a redistribution system that
most Texans know by its nickname — Robin Hood — and that the state's
own statutes call "recapture."

Recapture is one of the most consequential, most discussed, and most
misunderstood pieces of Texas public finance. A small set of
districts pay a great deal of money into it; a much larger set
benefit from it; and the politics of both groups are loud. Eanes
ISD, in west Austin, is the largest per-pupil recapture payer in the
state. By recent estimates it sends roughly seventy cents of every
M&O property tax dollar it collects to the state's recapture pool.

This post is a slow walk through how that works. It is the same
explainer voice as last week's post on [how a Texas property tax
bill is built]({{ '/posts/2026/06/property-tax-101/' | relative_url }}) — the goal is to leave a reader who has never
seen the recapture formula able to understand any of the policy
fights about it, regardless of which side they end up on.

## The constitutional logic

The Texas constitution requires the state to provide an "efficient
system of public free schools." In *Edgewood ISD v. Kirby* (1989),
the Texas Supreme Court interpreted that requirement to mean
something stronger than "every district gets a school." It meant
that *students of substantially equal effort should have access to
substantially equal funding*. If two districts both taxed themselves
at $1.00 per $100 of taxable value, they should be able to spend
roughly the same per pupil — even if one district had ten times the
taxable value per pupil that the other had.

That principle is the engine that drives the recapture system. Texas
districts vary enormously in property wealth per pupil. Eanes ISD,
in 2023, had roughly $4.5 million in taxable property value per
weighted student. A property-poor rural district might have
$200,000 per weighted student — a twenty-fold difference. Without
some intervention, a $1.00 tax rate in Eanes would raise twenty
times what the same rate would raise in the rural district. The
state's response is to let Eanes adopt whatever rate its voters
approve, but to recapture the revenue above a certain yield per
pupil and redistribute it.

## Where each Eanes dollar goes

The cleanest way to see what recapture does is to follow a dollar.
For every dollar Eanes ISD collects in M&O property tax in a recent
year:

![Where each Eanes M&O dollar goes]({{ '/images/eanes/eanes_dollar.png' | relative_url }})

Roughly thirty cents stays inside the district. Roughly seventy
cents is sent to the state, where it joins the recapture pool and
flows out to fund other districts through the school finance
formula. The exact share moves year to year — recapture went down
in 2020–2021 because of HB 3 compression and pandemic federal aid —
but the shape is consistent: Eanes is one of the very small number
of districts where the majority of locally raised tax revenue does
not stay in the district.

It is important to be careful about what this thirty-cent figure
*means*, because it is easy to misread.

- It is not Eanes' total per-pupil revenue. The district receives
  significant additional funding from federal Title sources, state
  programs, local-option I&S (debt service), and the "Golden
  Pennies" tier of M&O that we will get to in a moment. Total
  per-pupil revenue is closer to the $15,000 range, comparable to
  many suburban districts.
- It is the share of *local M&O property tax* that the district
  keeps. That distinction is what makes it politically charged:
  Eanes residents see the dollar leaving and feel that it is
  "their" dollar. The state's argument is that the dollar was
  raised under a state-authorized rate, on a state-equalized
  base, for a state-defined purpose.

## The recapture trend at the state level

Statewide, recapture grew steadily through the 2010s, peaked in the
2018–2019 tax year, and has slowly declined since:

![Recapture peaked in 2019 and has slowly fallen since]({{ '/images/eanes/eanes_statewide.png' | relative_url }})

The 2019 peak and subsequent decline are not coincidental. HB 3
(86th Legislature) compressed the Tier 1 M&O rate statewide. Lower
rates on the same taxable values yield less levy, which means less
to recapture. The state effectively traded "more state revenue to
schools" for "less recapture pressure on wealthy districts." The
state revenue side came from sales tax, severance, and federal
pandemic funds; the recapture side fell from about $3.1 billion to
about $2.4 billion.

The political pressure to weaken recapture further is constant and
bipartisan. Property-wealthy districts argue that recapture
constrains their ability to fund competitive teacher salaries.
Property-poor districts argue that recapture is what allows them to
hire teachers at all. The state legislature has spent the better
part of three decades trying to find a politically stable
equilibrium.

## Who actually pays

Eanes is the iconic recapture-paying district, but it is not the
largest in dollar terms — that title belongs to Austin ISD, which
captures a much larger commercial tax base and so writes a larger
check, even though its per-pupil property wealth is lower. The top
payers, in approximate recent dollars:

![Top recapture payers]({{ '/images/eanes/eanes_top_payers.png' | relative_url }})

Austin ISD writes the biggest check because it has the largest
property-wealth-times-students product. Highland Park ISD and
Eanes ISD send a higher *share* of their collections because they
have the highest property wealth per pupil. Different metrics,
different politics. When an Austin ISD bond fails, recapture is
often part of the case the no-side makes ("our tax money goes to
other districts anyway"). When an Eanes ISD bond fails, recapture
is sometimes part of the case the yes-side makes ("we are already
sending 70 cents on the dollar away, the bond is the only way to
fund what stays here").

There is a careful version of both arguments and a sloppy version,
and the press coverage usually amplifies the sloppy version. The
careful version requires understanding what the tier structure
actually does.

## The tier structure: Tier 1, Golden Pennies, Copper Pennies

This is the part of the system that almost nobody understands on
first reading, because the names sound like marketing slogans.
They are not. They are precise statutory categories with different
yield guarantees and different recapture rules.

![The Texas school M&O rate is built in three tiers]({{ '/images/eanes/eanes_tiers.png' | relative_url }})

**Tier 1** is the base M&O rate, recently compressed to roughly
$0.85 per $100 of taxable value statewide. Tier 1 is subject to
recapture: above the equalized yield per pupil, the state recaptures.

**Tier 2** is an additional set of pennies on top of Tier 1, capped
by statute. Tier 2 is split into two groups by yield guarantee and
recapture status:

- The first eight cents of Tier 2 are called **Golden Pennies**
  because they are not subject to recapture. A district that
  adopts the full eight cents keeps all of the revenue from those
  pennies, regardless of its property wealth.
- The next nine cents (up to seventeen cents of Tier 2 in total)
  are called **Copper Pennies**, and these *are* subject to
  recapture, with a lower yield guarantee than Tier 1.

This tier structure is what makes the politics so subtle. A
property-wealthy district can vote to add Golden Pennies and keep
every dollar of the new revenue. The same district adding Copper
Pennies above eight cents will be partly recaptured. A
property-poor district adding Golden Pennies will be partly
state-equalized up. The tiers create incentives for districts to
adopt rates up to the Golden Penny cap, and to be cautious about
crossing into Copper Penny territory unless their bond capacity
forces them to.

A reasonable summary: the state has been engineering the rate
structure to put as much M&O revenue as possible into rate ranges
that the state controls, and to leave Golden Pennies as the local
flexibility valve. Whether you think that engineering is
appropriate depends on your priors about local control vs. state
equalization — but the engineering is real and is the substance of
most school finance legislation in the last decade.

## Per-pupil math: what Eanes actually has to work with

Because the rate-times-base story is so different across districts,
the most legible way to compare them is per-pupil M&O revenue: how
much each district can actually spend per student after the
recapture math runs.

![Eanes collects double the state median; keeps less per pupil]({{ '/images/eanes/eanes_per_pupil.png' | relative_url }})

What this chart says is the heart of the recapture debate. Eanes
collects about $22,000 per pupil in M&O property tax — roughly
double the state median. After recapture, the district has about
$9,400 per pupil to spend on operations from M&O sources. The state
median district has about $10,500. So the system *is*, by design,
producing roughly equal per-pupil M&O revenue across districts of
very different property wealth.

There are two honest reactions to this chart, and they correspond
to the two angles on the recapture debate.

- **The pro-recapture reading:** "Look — it works. A district with
  twenty times the property wealth per pupil ends up with about the
  same per-pupil M&O revenue as the median district. That is
  exactly what the Texas Supreme Court said the constitution
  required."
- **The recapture-skeptic reading:** "But the median is the wrong
  comparison. Eanes voters are taxing themselves at a rate that, in
  a non-recapture state, would fund a much richer educational
  program. The system is essentially telling them they cannot use
  their local tax base for their local schools."

Both readings are coherent. Both are honestly held. The empirical
question of which districts are better off — and which students are
better off — under different recapture regimes is the subject of a
large school finance literature. The political question of which
reading should drive policy is what the legislature wrestles with
every session.

## What the reform debate actually pulls on

When you read a news story about "recapture reform" in a future
legislative session, the substantive levers being debated are
usually these:

1. The Tier 1 compression formula. How much of the M&O rate the
   state buys down with general revenue (which means more state aid
   and less local levy to recapture).
2. The Tier 2 Golden Penny / Copper Penny split. Specifically: how
   many additional Golden Pennies are allowed before pennies become
   subject to recapture.
3. The equalized yield-per-pupil ratios. How high above the state
   average a district can be before recapture kicks in, and the
   slope of the recapture formula above that threshold.
4. The list of exclusions. Specifically, whether certain
   district-specific costs (English-learner programs, special
   education, fast-growth construction) can be deducted from
   recapture obligations.

Each of these is a different policy lever with different
distributional consequences. A bill that "ends Robin Hood" by
buying down Tier 1 more aggressively (lever 1) is very different
from a bill that "ends Robin Hood" by lifting the Golden Penny cap
(lever 2) or by lowering the threshold yield (lever 3). The
substance of the bill matters far more than the slogan.

## Why Eanes keeps coming up

Eanes ISD is the iconic case for two reasons that have nothing to
do with the district itself. First, it has the highest property
wealth per pupil in the state, which makes it the largest
*per-pupil* recapture payer. Second, it is in the Austin media
market, which means its bond elections, school board fights, and
recapture frustrations get covered by *Austin American-Statesman*
and *Austin Monitor* reporters who can credibly explain the math
to a statewide audience.

Other Chapter 49 districts pay more in dollar terms (Austin ISD)
or have similar per-pupil wealth (Highland Park, Carroll). But
Eanes is the cleanest case study, because the gap between
collections and net is so large that the math is hard to miss.
That is why a post on recapture mechanics ends up being a post
about Eanes — not because Eanes is unusual in the system, but
because the system is so legible there.
