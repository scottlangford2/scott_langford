When a Texas homeowner opens their tax bill in October, they see a
single number at the bottom. What that number actually represents
is a long chain of separate decisions, made by a half-dozen
different governments that the homeowner mostly cannot name from
memory. Most of those decisions happened months earlier, in public
hearings that almost nobody attended.

This post is a slow walk through that chain. It is not the version
that gets you angry at any particular government. It is the version
that lets you understand what changing one number — the appraised
value, the homestead exemption, the school M&O rate, the city's
no-new-revenue rate — would actually do to the bottom of your bill.
The hope is that by the end, the property tax system feels less like
a single hostile force and more like what it is: a stack of
independently governed decisions that happen to land in the same
envelope.

I write about Texas state and local public finance for a living,
which means I spend a fair amount of my professional life trying to
explain this stack of decisions to people. The explanation is
genuinely complicated — Texas has roughly 4,400 taxing units, more
than any other state — but the underlying logic is not. Once you
see the components, the bill stops being a black box.

## A Texas property tax bill is not one tax

The first thing to know is that there is no such thing as "the
Texas property tax." There is a state law that governs how local
governments may levy property taxes, but the state itself does not
collect a property tax. Every dollar on your bill goes to a local
unit of government.

For a typical owner-occupied home in a Texas suburb, the bill is
the sum of four buckets:

- A **school district** tax, the biggest line, split between
  operations (M&O) and bond debt service (I&S).
- A **county** tax, which funds the courts, sheriff, jail, road
  & bridge work, and indigent healthcare.
- A **city** tax (if the property is inside city limits), which
  funds police, fire, parks, and general operations.
- A bundle of **other** taxes — community college, hospital
  district, emergency services district (ESD), municipal utility
  district (MUD), groundwater conservation district, sometimes a
  transit authority — each governed by its own elected board.

A hypothetical $350,000 home in a typical suburb produces something
that looks like this:

![Where the bill goes on a $350,000 Texas home]({{ '/images/property-tax/ptax_bill_split.png' | relative_url }})

The school bucket is the largest by a wide margin — usually 50–60
percent of the total — and the "other" bucket is where the
surprises live, because most homeowners do not know how many
overlapping districts they belong to. In Hays County, a parcel
inside Buda city limits, in Hays CISD, also pays a county tax, an
ESD tax, an Austin Community College tax, and (in many subdivisions)
a MUD tax. That is six bills in one envelope. In an exurban Harris
County subdivision with its own MUD, the count can hit nine.

![How many governments tax a single Texas home?]({{ '/images/property-tax/ptax_taxing_units.png' | relative_url }})

This is one of the things that makes the Texas property tax feel
unmanageable to homeowners: when the bill goes up, it is not
obvious which government to be angry at. The school district could
have held its rate flat while the MUD raised its rate by a third
to cover a new bond — and the homeowner sees only the total.

## Appraised, assessed, taxable: three different numbers

The bill starts with a value. In Texas, the value is set by the
county appraisal district, an independent local entity governed by
a board appointed by the taxing units in the county. The appraisal
district is required by law to value every property at 100 percent
of market value as of January 1 of the tax year.

That "100 percent of market" requirement matters. In a fast-rising
market — which is what most of central Texas has been since roughly
2013 — appraised values can rise faster than household income. Two
mechanisms partly cushion that:

1. **The homestead cap.** For a property the owner claims as their
   primary residence, the **assessed** value can rise no more than
   10 percent per year, regardless of what the market did. So if
   your home was appraised at $300,000 last year and the appraisal
   district says it is worth $400,000 this year, your *assessed*
   value (the one you actually pay tax on) is capped at $330,000.
   The difference accumulates as "capped value" — and it snaps
   back to full market value the moment you sell.

2. **Exemptions.** Once the assessed value is set, exemptions are
   subtracted to produce the **taxable** value. The big one is the
   residence homestead exemption: as of the 2023 constitutional
   amendment, the school portion of the exemption is $100,000 for
   the school taxing unit. County, city, and special districts may
   each adopt their own local-option homestead exemption (often
   $25,000 or 20 percent of value, whichever is greater). There are
   also exemptions for those over 65, those who are disabled, and
   disabled veterans.

So a single home has three different values floating around at the
same time:

- **Appraised** value (market, set by the appraisal district).
- **Assessed** value (after the homestead cap, if applicable).
- **Taxable** value (after exemptions; *different for each taxing
  unit*, because each has its own exemption schedule).

The "different for each taxing unit" part is important. Your
school-portion taxable value is your assessed value minus
$100,000. Your county-portion taxable value is your assessed value
minus the county's local-option exemption, which might be 20
percent of the assessed value, or might be zero. Your MUD-portion
taxable value typically has no homestead exemption at all. Same
home, three different bases.

## The rates: M&O, I&S, and the no-new-revenue benchmark

Once each taxing unit has its own taxable value, the rest is
arithmetic: taxable value times rate equals the tax. Rates in
Texas are quoted in dollars per $100 of taxable value. A rate of
$0.95 per $100 means a $3,325 school-tax bill on $350,000 of
taxable value.

Every taxing unit's rate is the sum of two pieces:

- **M&O** (maintenance & operations) — pays for the operating
  budget. Salaries, supplies, fuel, contracts.
- **I&S** (interest & sinking) — pays the debt service on bonds
  that voters approved at past elections. The taxing unit cannot
  reduce this rate without first defeasing the bond.

The split matters because they have different politics. M&O is
the rate that can go up or down each year by the same governing
body that sets it. I&S is essentially predetermined by the bond
schedule — if voters approved a $200 million school bond in 2018,
the I&S rate must be set high enough each year to make that year's
debt payment. So when a city or school district says "we held the
rate flat," they may mean they held the M&O flat while the I&S
rose because of a recently issued bond.

Two pieces of state law constrain these rates:

- **The no-new-revenue rate (NNR)** is the rate that, if applied
  to this year's taxable value, would produce the same total levy
  as last year (on the same properties). It is the political
  benchmark — adopting a rate above it is, in effect, a tax
  increase even if the rate number went down.
- **The voter-approval rate (VAR)** is the rate above which the
  taxing unit must hold an automatic election to ratify the
  increase. Since SB 2 (2019), this cap is 3.5 percent above
  no-new-revenue for cities and counties, and 2.5 percent for
  school district M&O. Before SB 2, the cap was 8 percent and
  required a petition to trigger a rollback election rather than
  an automatic one.

The combination of these two benchmarks is what gives the
Truth-in-Taxation hearings their structure: every taxing unit has
to compute and publish its NNR and VAR, and adopt a rate by formal
vote with public notice. The 2019 reforms tightened the math; they
did not change the basic ritual.

## What the 2019 reforms actually changed

The reform package that the 86th Legislature passed in 2019 — SB 2
on the cities-and-counties side, HB 3 on the school finance side —
was the largest property tax overhaul in a generation. It is worth
understanding what each piece did separately, because they
operated on different parts of the bill.

**SB 2** was about the *growth rate* of the levy. It lowered the
voter-approval rate from 8 percent to 3.5 percent above
no-new-revenue for cities and counties, and made the election
automatic rather than petition-driven. It carved out small taxing
units (under $500K in M&O levy), debt service, new value, and
disaster years — meaning the 3.5 percent cap applies only to the
M&O levy on existing properties.

The data show what the cap was meant to do — and what it could not
do during a pandemic-driven appraisal surge:

![Texas tried to slow the levy treadmill in 2019]({{ '/images/property-tax/ptax_levy_growth.png' | relative_url }})

The post-2019 dip is visible but modest. The cap slows the M&O
piece; it does nothing to the I&S piece (because debt service is
carved out), and it does nothing to growth from new construction
(also carved out). In a county adding thousands of new homes each
year, the *total* levy keeps rising even while the *rate* falls.
That is one reason homeowners often feel like SB 2 didn't deliver
relief: their individual bill kept going up because their
individual assessed value kept rising.

**HB 3** was about the *level* of the school M&O rate. It
"compressed" the M&O rate statewide — meaning the state agreed to
pay more per pupil out of the state budget, which let school
districts lower their M&O rate without losing operating revenue.
The compression formula has tightened in each subsequent session,
and the result is visible in the M&O share of the school rate:

![HB 3 compressed school M&O; debt service kept growing]({{ '/images/property-tax/ptax_mo_is.png' | relative_url }})

Two things are happening in that chart. The M&O rate (the blue
band) is falling because of state-funded compression. The I&S rate
(the yellow band) is rising because districts continue to pass
bonds for new schools, particularly in fast-growing suburbs. The
*total* school rate has come down, but not as much as the
compression alone would suggest — because debt service is silently
filling in some of the space that compression vacated.

## Why Texas property tax burdens are so high

The state-level statistic that gets used most often in policy
conversations is the **effective tax rate**: total property tax
paid as a share of owner-occupied home value. By that measure,
Texas is consistently in the top six or seven states in the
country, depending on the year and the methodology.

![Texas has one of the heaviest property tax burdens in the U.S.]({{ '/images/property-tax/ptax_state_compare.png' | relative_url }})

The reason is structural and well known: Texas has no state income
tax. The state constitution prohibits one without a constitutional
amendment, and there is no political constituency for amending it.
Public services in Texas — schools especially, but also roads,
public safety, and (in many places) emergency medical services —
have to be paid for somehow, and the property tax is what is
politically available at the local level. The sales tax does a
share of the work at the state level (and is high by national
standards), and there are oil and gas severance taxes, but the
local-services bill mostly falls on real property.

That structural fact is worth holding onto when reading any
"property tax reform" debate. A reform that lowers the rate
without lowering the spending on the other side has to come from
somewhere. HB 3 came from the state's general revenue — which
ultimately came from sales tax, oil & gas, and federal pandemic
funds. SB 2 did not change the spending side at all; it just
slowed the growth rate of the levy and made the political process
of raising rates more visible.

## What this means for the rest of the blog

The next several Southbound 35 posts are going to be about specific
applications of this system: how a fast-growing suburb finances its
schools (Eanes, Leander, Hays CISD), how an interstate corridor
shapes which taxing units exist where, what happens when a major
employer's appraised value swings 30 percent in a single year, and
how the recapture (Robin Hood) system redistributes school
revenue across districts. All of that work runs through the
mechanics above — appraisal, exemption, rate, M&O vs. I&S, NNR vs.
VAR. So this post will be a footnote on every one of them.

If you take one thing away: when you next see a story about
property taxes, ask which of the four levers the policy actually
pulls. Is it changing the appraisal cap? The exemption? The M&O
rate cap? The bond approval threshold? Each of those changes does
something different, and the reform debate is often muddier than
it needs to be because all four get talked about as if they were
the same lever.
