Public finance, when the institutions are the constraint.

I study state and local public finance, economic development, and how institutions absorb — or fail to absorb — fast growth. I write Southbound 35, a blog on Texas's I-35 corridor.

About

A short version

I am an assistant professor in the Department of Political Science at Texas State University, in the Masters of Public Administration program. I previously served as a post-doctoral research fellow in the School of Public Affairs at Arizona State University, and earned my PhD in Public Policy at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill.

My research uses public administration and public policy frameworks together with rigorous research designs and applied econometric methods to answer policy-relevant questions. Substantively, I work at the intersection of economics, public health, and the environment — particularly how local economies absorb shocks like extreme weather and what moderates those effects.

My independent research has been funded by the Kenan Institute for Private Enterprise. Recent work has appeared in the Journal of Business Venturing Insights, Economic Development Quarterly, the Journal of Regional Science, and JAMA Pediatrics.

Selected paper

Young and Small Firms and Resilience to Extreme Weather Events

Heinzel, Langford, Peters & Sanders — Journal of Business Venturing Insights, 2025

Counties with a larger employment share in young and small firms experience smaller employment losses for a given level of property damage from extreme weather events. Entrepreneurship is a public good for resilience.
